A Proposal for Union County, North Carolina

Blue Envelope.

A simple, voluntary tool that helps law enforcement officers communicate effectively with autistic drivers during traffic stops. Already law in nine states. Already running in Dare County, NC. Not yet in Union County.

Read the Proposal → See the Cost →
The Tool Itself

It's just
an envelope.

A standard #10 blue envelope. On the front: the driver's license, registration, and insurance — plus brief notes for the officer. On the back: tips for both the driver and the officer about how to communicate during a stop. Free, voluntary, and self-contained. Nothing high-tech. Nothing expensive. Nothing that requires a database or registry.

A typical Blue Envelope as used in active state programs.

04 / NC Precedent

Dare County Did It First.

One person. Outer Banks Health Speech Language Pathologist Kristin Kuhar saw the program in another state and brought it home.

Seven agencies. Within months she built a coalition of every police department in Dare County plus the Sheriff’s Office.

One year. The program launched in July 2024 with free envelopes at police lobbies, libraries, medical practices, senior centers, and high schools.

No new law required. Voluntary. Self-implemented. Already running.

06 / How It Works

Three Steps.
That's It.

No registry. No proof of diagnosis required. No database of participants. The Blue Envelope is voluntary, free, and designed to fit inside an existing traffic stop with zero workflow change for officers.

01

Pick Up

Driver picks up a free blue envelope at any participating Union County PD lobby, library, or DMV pop-up event. No registration, no proof of diagnosis required.

02

Prepare

Driver places copies of license, registration, and insurance inside the envelope, along with optional emergency contacts and personal communication notes.

03

Present

During a traffic stop, the driver hands the envelope to the officer. The officer immediately knows to slow down, reduce sensory input, and use single-step instructions.

14 shots in under 2 seconds.

— Pocatello, Idaho, April 5, 2025. Victor Perez, 17, was a nonspeaking autistic Latino teen with intellectual disability and cerebral palsy. Officers fired 14 rounds in under two seconds. Twelve struck him. He died. The Idaho Attorney General declined to file charges, citing that “the officers were not familiar with Perez’s limited capabilities.” His family filed a federal ADA lawsuit in June 2025.
07 / Roadmap

Five Phases.
Twelve Weeks.

Implementation can move from kickoff to public launch in approximately three months, using existing law enforcement and community channels. No legislation required.

Phase 01
2-3 weeks
Stakeholders & Scope
Convene Union County Sheriff, Monroe PD, Waxhaw PD, Marshville PD, Wingate PD, county manager, local hospitals, school SRO leads, and Autism Society of NC. Adopt voluntary, no-registry program policy.
Phase 02
2-4 weeks
Materials
Design and print #10 blue envelopes with bilingual driver tips and officer tips. Use Connecticut, Massachusetts, and Virginia phrasing as a base. Source via county procurement (3 quotes).
Phase 03
1-2 weeks prep
Training
30-45 minute roll-call briefing for every sworn officer. Repeat for new hires. One-page handout, optional 5-minute video. Layered over existing CIT training where applicable.
Phase 04
2-3 weeks
Distribution & Launch
Pick-up points at all PD lobbies, county libraries, DMV events, autism support groups. Joint press conference with sheriff, chiefs, and advocates. Stock every patrol car with spare envelopes.
Phase 05
Ongoing
Metrics & Annual Report
Track envelopes distributed, training delivered, officer recall quizzes, community surveys. Annual report to the Board of Commissioners. Use data to support a future statewide North Carolina bill.
